1. Conveying a Clear Message

The first chapter introduces the basics for planning a discussion or a talk. Starting from the main message, because most of the times people don’t even know what they want to achieve. Whenever you are participating in a discussion with someone, craft a clear message that you want to stick in your interlocutor’s mind. One simple message that respects those rules:

  • Short (ideally 10 words long)
  • Using Simple Language
  • Focused on the needs of audience: what they need to know and do from this information?

And a couple of bonus tips: Remember, it’s about them, not you. When beginning a conversation, you can start with what’s in front of you for any topic to discuss, and continue the conversation involving the other person. Another useful tip is when people ask what you do for a living, do not just tell your title, but whgat you do. So, no “BI Analyst” but rather “I design and build strategic reports”

2. Telling Engaging Stories

People love stories, stories are the things that get stuck in our mind because it’s a pttern that our brain has learned to follow. So, to convey our messages, doing it as a story will make it 10, 100 times more powerful. Storytelling is a powerful weapon at our disposal.

Sullivan provides example of a successful storytelling, starting from the fact that Every story has a beginning, middle and a end.

  • Beginning: the setup, such us “Last week I was in the office”: where and when the action happens
  • Middle√ß the sturggle, building tension. Only include the crucial details
  • The end, the final takeaway. It should be a consequence of the middle section, you can even highlight hath the point of the story is this one

Also, some ground rules. Do not go negative, do not make fun of other people, don’t try to be funny. Unless you are confident in your skills, don’t exaggerate.

3. Organizing Your Content

Now that you have the message, you have to plan your strategy. There are three aspects to consider:

  1. The audience’s needs
  2. The key message (see Chapter 1)
  3. Your purpose

The first point is crucial for getting the attention of the listener. People don’t care about you at first, so you need to immediately give them what they are here for. Example: you have to talk with your boss. You should begin with what you know it will set him in the right mood, e.g. “Everything is on track” or “We have a problem”. Set the convertation immediately, then proceed with your message.

For longer discussions, such a presentation, Sullivan provides a couple of structural architectures:

  • Informative Roadmap: classic structure with Context, Agenda, Topic 1,2,3, FInal Summary and Next Steps
  • Persuasive Roadmap: used for convincing someone, it uses Context, Recommendation (key message first!), Benefit, Prove Benefit 1,2,3, Final Summary and Next Steps

Tip: focus on the key message first, not the opening. That’s what the presentation is all about and will help overcome the writer block

5. Listening to Understand (‚≠ê)

Again, the focus is on the others, not us. How can we focus? By listening! Listening well takes energy, and we need to show to the counterpart that we are well attentive and we care about what they say. Being a good listener ism ranked as the number 1 skill for a top leader. Eye contact, taking notes, asking probing questions, even the standard “mhh mhh” are all body language clues of someone who is interested in the conversation.

But listening is not only for the others, but for you, because you listen, and in gneeral, ask things to people to search infomrations you didn’t even know you didn’t know. to get this, you can leveverage open-ended questions like “How” and “Why” that ask the other person to reflect and give a complex response, or closed-ended questions that require just a yes/no sand receive a very precise response. Usually, giving closed-ended question is a closing tecnique in sales situations, but be aware to not self-censor yourself with questions like “Any other challenges with this issue?”: in this case, it’s very easy to just say no. Use the open ended alternative like “What other challenges you see?”. This will help you drilling down until you reach what you didn’t even klnow you didn’t know.

Another final point is that our job is to help people, so after having listened to them, we need to make sure we are truly helpful. ‚≠ê 3 ways on how to be helpful to remember:

  1. How can I help? The basic to show you listened and you want to give support
  2. What would be most helpful to you? A very effective way to receive some genuine and precise feedbacks from the other person
  3. Would it be helpful if I…? If someone is lost, this can give him/her some clues about what could be the next step

We all make assumptions: to confirm what you have heard, reinstate the cocnept with an introductory phrase such as “So, if I’m hearing you correctly…” or “I think you’ve shared”, a restatement of the point made and a confirming question like “am I right”? This will avoid confusion.

6. Delivering from Notes and Visuals (‚≠ê)

When using notes or a presentation, your main job is to make it effortless for your adueicne to recieve the information. The strategy to keep the attention high is always eye contact, so you can follow this pattern:

  1. See it: look a the bullet point in the slide and read it all
  2. Save it: remember the content as you look up from the notes
  3. Say it: say the point when looking at an individual in the audience

‚≠ê The Arc of Silence: the arc is the distance from the eyes of someone in the audience you are targeting back to the point you are reading and back again to another pair of eyes. Don’t talk during that arc. ALL SOUND IS DELIVERED TO A PAIR OF EYES.√π

This has the beneift of maitaning the power by coming across as comfortable with pauses, give time to the audience to digest what you have said, and then giving space to your line beforee sharing the most important information (which we usually place until the end of the sentence).

PAy attention to the posture: use gestures to directly point to your points, stand with your back parallel to the back wall of the room: all the attnetion will be to the audience! Keep your feet planted in one spot, only pros walk around the stage.

About slides: the trick sullivan says is to read back what’s on the slide to the audience, and then add what’s NOT on each point. If it’s a list of 3 bullet points, you can use the Arc of Silence, read each point, add more context, go to the second point, add second context, and go on. You need to cover everything, and of course keep all the points simple. We read every word in the slide is usually a bad sign, but if you do like that, you will fix everything in the memory of please as they have two inpouts: voice and visual, saying the same thing.

Balancing your slide deck can be challenging: opt for a streamlined presentation and provide a more detailed slide deck as a handout afterward. You can’t have a highly detailed document and a compelling presentation simultaneously; it’s essential to separate the two.

7. Responding to Questions (‚ùì)

When someone asks a question, they are revealing something about themselves and their perspective on the topic. It takes courage to do this, especially in large audiences. Show respect and follow this four-step process to respond:

  1. Listen to the entire question: allow the person to finish their question without interrupting.
  2. Gain time to think: maintain eye contact but take a moment to gather your thoughts. You can buy time by repeating the question or using a lead-in like “Great question” or “Interesting”. Avoid this if the question is an accusation or very direct, as it may seem insincere
  3. Answer and reaffirm the main pont
  4. Ask for the next question: conclude with “Any other questions?”

8. Editing for Clarity

Always ask yourself:

  • Why is the reader reading this document?
  • What does the reader need to do with the information I’m sharing?
  • How can I make it effortless for the reader to get the main message?

It’s an art: get straight to the point. Eliminate unnecessary words, the so-called “Zero” words who do not add value to a sentence like “Currently” or “As previously mentioned”. General rule: each sentece should contain one idea. From then, decided what the key action is in the sentence and make it the verb, if it’s an istance of the verb “be” and it’s not astate, try to find a verb to convery the action. E.g. “he made a statement that he was quitting the race” becomes “he stated that he was quitting the race”.

About active or passive: usually you should use the active form which is catchier, unless for special cases in which you want to soften ascribing accoutnability to someone (e.g. “your request has been denied” is better than “we denied you permission”) or establish a sense of high authority (e.g. rules).

9. Structuring your Documents (‚ùì)

WHy are you writing this document? Focus on the benefits of taking your recommended course of action. You might have two different purposes, with their correspondant document structure:

  1. Persuading -> State Main Message, Give Background (only as few as needed), Expand Message, End Quickly
  2. Informing: -> Introduce Subject, State Topics, Explain Each Topic in Detail, Summarize

These frameworks align with the concepts discussed in Chapter 3.

10. Creating Reader-Friendly Documents

Beyond the structure, here is a miscellanus of tips to make your docs even more reader-friendly

  1. Pronouns: if you find a lof of sentences with I, chances are you are making the document more about you than the reader
  2. Maximum 17 words per sentneces. Over that limit, it’s harder to process the information
  3. Vary the sentence length: if it takes 20 words for that line, it’s fine. just make sure the next paragraph is less than 10 words.
  4. Don’t overwhelm the reader. Structure the document in smaller chunks.

11. Writing Emails That Resonate (‚ùì)

Some email tips that can be useful: structure the email starting from the title. It should convey the topic of the email, like “Alpha Proposal for Compliance”, not generic “Follow Up Call”. By starting with the name, .e.g. “Dear Lisa”, you can figure out the person you are speaking with, and better process how to structure the mail. Different people expect different emails.

Also, manage expectations: clearly indicate a deadline if it exists and mention the people you are referring to if there are multiple destinatairs.

13. Delegating Successfully

The most significant step we take in our growth is when we move from managign ourselves to managing others. With this change, not only we manage our deadlines and changes and struggle,s but multiply them for all the people under you. Sullivan shares information on how the be an effective boss: you need to be effective at work, not nice. This still means to follow the mantra of the book, focus on others, not yourself. If you delegate an assignment, it’s not about what you need, it’s about what the other person needs in order to give you want you want. There are 6 steps on how to delegate successfully:

  1. State the Big Picture: let the person who is working on an item know how important it will be. We all want our work to matter
  2. Determine Expectations: talk with the other person, clearlyt state what you want her to do
  3. Explain the Role of Others: given an overview of the stakeholders and the people that can support the person
  4. Explain the Reasons for Selecting This individual: Including the benefits to Him or her.
  5. Explain the Next Steps: how to get started and the overall initial structure of things to do
  6. Summarize What you Want the Person to Do

15. Giving Toasts

Very underrated chapter, but a toast is a unique setting. You are speaking in from of a crowd, but the real audience is only a person. You are telling the room the value he has brought there. Some simple rules:

  • Don’t fake it if you don’t know the person very well, nobody expects that. Just thank the person for his or her service
  • Don’t talk about you.
  • Avoid inside jokes if less than half of the audience can get them
  • Never go negative and avoid sarcasm
  • Remember that the theme is always how this person has impacted organization and their people
